02 What I built at TDK, from scratch

A full sensor validation platform, stood up end to end.

At TDK I did not inherit a test setup. I built one. I introduced the Vector toolchain at the site, constructed and virtualised the CAN networks, modelled the sensor systems and their subcomponents, wrote the embedded firmware, and recreated the sensor itself in two forms: in software and on silicon.

The result was a platform where sensor behaviour, communication and faults could all be reproduced on demand, the kind of controllable, trustworthy test environment that real machine autonomy depends on.

Sensor technology selection, 30+ technologies evaluated

Robert Bosch, 2024
Direct match: choosing and integrating robotic sensors

My master's thesis was a structured study of which sensor to trust for a real world measurement job. I evaluated more than 30 sensor technologies, including Radar, LiDAR and ultrasonic, against accuracy, integration effort, cost and feasibility using a weighted Pugh matrix, then built a controlled laboratory rig and characterised the chosen ultrasonic reference across tilt angles, temperatures and medium concentrations with data acquisition logging and Python analysis. The recommendation was adopted for series production. Selecting the right sensor for autonomy, and proving it under real conditions, is exactly this work.

05 A belief I will defend
An uncommon view on the future of robotics and autonomy

The field over invests in perception and under invests in the boring physical layer, and that is where real world autonomy will be won or lost.

The popular narrative says intelligence is the bottleneck: bigger models, more data, smarter end to end learning. My contrarian view, shaped by years validating sensors in harsh conditions, is that the limiting factor on real machines is not the algorithm. It is whether the sensor data stays clean and trustworthy when the hardware is shaken, heated and electrically noisy for years.

A brilliant Vision Language Action model fed by a sensor with a drifting calibration or a marginal connector is worse than a simpler system on rock solid hardware, because it fails in ways nobody can predict. The companies that win in heavy industry will treat physical sensor integration as a first class intelligence problem, not as plumbing. That is exactly why a Physical AI company that owns the hardware software boundary has the right idea.
